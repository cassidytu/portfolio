Problem

Climate change is a threat to our whole civilization, with effects that are already prominent and will continue to grow to catastrophic heights unless action is taken now. As one of the biggest challenges to tackle in today’s society, world leaders have come together and agreed on a plan to make the world a greener, fairer, and better place by 2030. But as members of society that necessarily involved, how would we also take steps to help this plan?

How can we make the knowledge needed to combat climate change more accessible and engaging for the general public?

Solution

EcoHero was created as a way to help motivate the average person to integrate more eco-conscious actions in their daily lives. Through encouragement and the visualization of impact on climate change and providing easy ways to get involved, EcoHero seeks to make climate change less daunting to combat!

Recommending Actions

Suggests possible courses of actions pertaining to a certain goal of fighting climate change

Makes involvement easier for users and streamlines the process of finding resources

Tracking Impact

Tracks logged climate action efforts of user and visualizes their impact

Encourages users and minimize their feelings of helplessness

Research

I made my notes according to Jakob Nielson's 10 Usability Heuristics and Nielsen Norman Group's Severity Ratings for Usability Problems.

Competitor Analysis

Focusing on two major government funded websites, whose main purpose is to serve the general public, I conducted a heuristic evaluation to identify their issues and how to fix these flaws to attract more of this demographic.

Competitor Usability Test

I conducted a test on the websites to gather live feedback and further solidify my previous research, as well as practice preparing and facilitating interview sessions.

Contextual Inquiry

Coming into this project, I was set on redesigning the CalEPA website. However, this contextual inquiry allowed me to revisit the root problem I'm trying to address, the discoverability of climate action and incentivization of it. My participant provided a different perspective as an individual who wasn't really interested exploring these basic resources of climate action.

So I began to wonder, how can I encourage users and make climate action as easy and fun as possible?

Key Takeaways

01

Lack of Motivation

Users need extrinsic and intrinsic motivations to encourage their actions, as well as a way to visualize the impact their actions have to lessen the feeling of helplessness.

"I don't really pay attention [to climate action] because I don't see the results"

"I'm just one individual, I don't necessarily believe I could make a dent"

02

Efficiency and Engagement

Previous websites were difficult for users to search for specific information with only a single journey path to reach it. Users also found these platforms to be "busy" and too text heavy to be interesting.

"That task took a bit longer than I had expected it to"

"It was boring and I didn't even look at all of it"

Storytelling

With UX storytelling through multiple components (such as personas, empathy maps, scenarios, and journey maps), I was able to put myself into the perspective of the users by considering all these facets of these personas that are representative of the targeted audience. I humanized the user and their story, pain points, and behavior, ultimately leading to a better focus on their needs/goals and serve as the foundation for the features of my product that would solve these issues.

Ideation

Low Fidelity +
Cognitive Walkthrough

The prototyping allowed me to test the flow and functionality of the features that I had conceptualized. I had a few users conduct a cognitive walkthrough the sketches, documented with the colored arrows, to ensure I wasn't missing any pain points.

Design System

This interface design brought the previous paper, low-fi prototype to life! I tested different typography, shape, and color variations and ran color contrast tests to ensure everything was visually accessible. Impression and preference tests were also conducted to help with looking at the overall image of the design.

Cognitive Walkthroughs

I conducted more walkthroughs with my prototypes and using all of the feedback I had gotten, I proceeded to make even more changes during this round of iterations.

User #02: "not able to click back to calendar"

User #03: "can't go back to change the date when signing up for the clean up"

I made a few different versions to see what would be the most discoverable. The best solution was to place the back action at the bottom near the sign up button, which was decided through a preference test as most users liked this version because it was more clear.

Users prioritize flexibility and discoverability.

User #04: "pie chart being gradient makes it challenging to see how much each category takes"

User #02: "the emissions graph on the profile page is a little hard to interpret"

This is an instance of where the designer (me) has prioritized aesthetics over functionality. I realized that having solid, clear is easier to read. Other changes to include is a hover feature that will show a breakdown of each section as colors may still be vague and hard to precisely interpret.

Users prioritize clarity.

Usability Testing

With a more polished high-fidelity prototype, I felt ready to move onto usability testing with a product that has a clear purpose and understand flows between interactions and features. I conducted usability test (UT) to gather live feedback from users by observing them while they interact with the product.

User #01: Hovered over impact rating expecting something

Placing the impact info pop-up near the impact rating was more intuitive for users. They prefer it as gives more insight into their question of how the impact is calculated. They also mentioned how it is nice to be able to see the information prior to completion of the action.

Users prioritize satisfying their curiosity instantly.

*These were just a few of the changes I made with the additional testing. As I continue with more iterations, I'm sure there will be more things I can improve upon!

Reflections

01

Research is Key

Learning how a heuristic evaluation provides important, yet different, data compared to a contextual inquiry allowed me to have insight in a multitude of aspects ranging from user behavior to more technical rules of thumb.

02

Design Preparations

This was my first time creating a design system myself. A cohesive foundation of what spacing, font size, etc. is the backbone of having internal consistency to project design. While time consuming to set up at first, implementing all the details into my design file, it was very helpful during the hi-fi prototyping process and sharing my files with others.
